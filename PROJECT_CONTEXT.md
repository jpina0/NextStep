# Project Context

## Objective

The objective of this project is to propose a path towards more robust and efficient democracies, where efficiency is understood as the ability of a political system to protect and improve the well-being of the society it governs.

The proposal is based on the observation that democratic systems suffer from two fundamental limitations:

1. Misalignment between the interests of government agents and the interests of society as a whole.
2. Limited competence of government agents, resulting from the inherent limitations of human beings.

A further difficulty arises from the fact that ordinary citizens generally lack the knowledge and expertise required to evaluate the performance of government agents. Effective evaluation often requires specialized knowledge. However, if evaluation is delegated to specialists, a new problem emerges: who evaluates the specialists?

The central proposal of this project is that these limitations can be mitigated through the adoption of new democratic values. These values should encourage citizens to seek informed evaluations from competent specialists while simultaneously creating incentives for those specialists to evaluate and supervise one another.

The project does not primarily seek institutional reform. Instead, it proposes a cultural and moral evolution in the values that guide democratic participation.

## Structure of the Project

The project is organized into three layers.

### Layer 1: Core

The Core is a highly condensed document of approximately ten pages.

Its purpose is not to provide extensive explanations or discuss objections. Instead, it presents the essential propositions upon which the project is built.

The writing style resembles that of a scientific manual or an axiomatic system, relying on:

* Postulates
* Definitions
* Hypotheses
* Theses

The Core is divided into four chapters.

#### Chapter 1: Moral

This chapter introduces a moral foundation that serves as the basis for the remainder of the project.

The chosen moral system is not presented as the only possible foundation, but as a suitable foundation for the arguments that follow.

#### Chapter 2: Human Nature

This chapter examines how human beings make decisions and the limitations they face when attempting to act according to the moral framework established in Chapter 1.

It introduces concepts such as intuition, moral values, reason, and the limitations of human judgment.

#### Chapter 3: The Role of the State

Using the concepts developed in the previous chapters, this chapter defines the purpose of the state.

The state is presented as a natural consequence of morally correct behavior and as a tool that assists individuals in the pursuit of their well-being.

#### Chapter 4: The Next Step

This chapter presents the central proposal of the project.

It argues that the values currently present in democratic societies are insufficient for effective governance and proposes the adoption of additional democratic values intended to improve the evaluation and supervision of government agents.

---

### Layer 2: First Q&A Layer

The first Q&A layer expands the content of the Core.

Its purpose is to:

* Explain the reasoning behind the postulates, definitions, hypotheses, and theses.
* Clarify concepts.
* Provide examples.
* Compare the proposed framework with alternative moral, political, or philosophical systems.
* Address foreseeable questions from readers.

The first Q&A layer should be viewed as explanatory material rather than as part of the Core itself.

---

### Layer 3: Second Q&A Layer

The second Q&A layer exists to challenge and improve the first Q&A layer.

Its purpose is to:

* Critique existing answers.
* Introduce objections.
* Question assumptions.
* Propose alternative interpretations.
* Suggest the inclusion of new questions.
* Suggest the removal of unnecessary questions.

This layer functions as a mechanism of continuous refinement and intellectual self-correction.

---

## Guiding Principle

The project is founded on the idea that human beings are limited in their ability to make correct decisions, particularly in complex domains such as governance.

Because individuals are vulnerable to ignorance, manipulation, and short-term incentives, improvements in democratic performance cannot rely solely on better institutions or better leaders.

Instead, they require the development of values that help individuals make better democratic decisions and create mechanisms through which competence can be identified, evaluated, and supervised.

The proposed democratic values are intended to serve this function.
